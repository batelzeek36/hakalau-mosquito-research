# Hakalau / Hamakua Coast Mosquito Conditions: Species, Regulation, and Conservation Context

Site: ~15-acre parcel near Hakalau, windward Big Island, ~15 mi N of Hilo. Plausible elevation range: sea level to ~6,500 ft on eastern Mauna Kea slope. Rainfall 150"+ at coast, >250" below 4,000 ft, ~150" above 4,500 ft (https://www.hawaiilife.com/blog/rainfall-and-climate-zones-big-island-hawaii/). All seven established Hawaii mosquito species are introduced (HISC: https://dlnr.hawaii.gov/hisc/mosquitos/).

## 1. Species inventory

The 2015–2016 Big Island dengue outbreak entomological survey is the cleanest published species composition for the Big Island. Of 3,259 trapped mosquitoes (Johnston et al. 2020, AJTMH; https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7124917/):

| Species | Share | Activity | Disease relevance |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Aedes albopictus* (Asian tiger) | 58% | Aggressive day-biter; sea level to ~4,000 ft | Primary 2015–16 dengue vector; spreads avian pox; nuisance |
| *Aedes aegypti* (yellow fever) | 25% | Day-biter, anthropophagic | Re-detected on Big Island; dengue/Zika-competent |
| *Wyeomyia mitchellii* (bromeliad) | 7% | Day-biter | Nuisance only; established 1981 (UH ScholarSpace: https://scholarspace.manoa.hawaii.edu/items/342191b1-70a5-4b00-bd42-c7b091803004) |
| *Aedes vexans* | 5% | Crepuscular | Floodwater nuisance |
| *Culex quinquefasciatus* (southern house) | 4% | Night-biter; flies up to ~1.6 km | **Sole avian-malaria vector**; West Nile-competent (Birds Not Mosquitoes: https://www.birdsnotmosquitoes.org/invasive-mosquitoes) |
| *Aedes japonicus* | 1% | Day/twilight | Container-breeder |

Important corrections to common assumptions:

- **Ae. aegypti is NOT eradicated.** It was eliminated by DDT in the 1940s–60s but has re-established in pockets of the Big Island (Hawaii DOH: https://health.hawaii.gov/docd/disease_listing/dengue/). The 2015–16 outbreak was vectored primarily by *Ae. albopictus*, but *Ae. aegypti* was 25% of the trap haul.
- **Culex is the smallest fraction of human-biting catch but the most ecologically dangerous mosquito on the property** because it is the only avian-malaria vector.
- **Wyeomyia mitchellii is essentially guaranteed on any Hakalau property with ornamental bromeliads, heliconia, or naturalized epiphytes** — it breeds only in leaf axils.

## 2. Avian malaria and the native bird argument

Hakalau Forest National Wildlife Refuge sits at 2,500–6,500 ft on the eastern Mauna Kea slope and protects the densest endemic forest bird assemblage in Hawai'i, including 'i'iwi, 'apapane, Hawai'i 'amakihi, 'akiapola'au, and 'akepa (Wikipedia / FWS: https://www.fws.gov/refuge/hakalau-forest). Avian malaria (*Plasmodium relictum*) transmitted by *Culex quinquefasciatus* is the primary driver of honeycreeper extinction (Cornell Wildlife Health: https://wildlife.cornell.edu/blog/landscape-level-mosquito-suppression-protect-hawaiis-rapidly-vanishing-avifauna). Other lowland mosquitoes — *Ae. albopictus*, *Wyeomyia* — are largely refractory to *Plasmodium* infection (USGS: https://www.usgs.gov/publications/comparative-susceptibility-introduced-forest-dwelling-mosquitoes-hawaii-avian-malaria).

**Elevation thresholds for Culex / malaria transmission** (Atkinson et al. PLOS One 2017, https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0168880; Paxton et al. 2016 *Science Advances*; Hakalau "moving castle" 2026 ScienceDirect: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2351989426000430):

- Below ~1,250 m (~4,100 ft): year-round Culex transmission, lethal to honeycreepers
- 1,500 m (4,920 ft): historical malaria boundary; declines now observed below 1,700 m
- 1,700–1,900 m (5,580–6,230 ft): currently stable
- Above 1,900 m (6,230 ft): the last refugia; trending warmer

**Implication by parcel elevation:**

- Coastal/lower Hakalau (sea level–2,000 ft): squarely in the year-round malaria transmission zone; Culex breeds continuously.
- Mid-slope (2,000–4,500 ft): in the climbing transition zone; warming has pushed Culex upward, shrinking honeycreeper habitat.
- Upper-slope (>4,500 ft, especially >5,580 ft): historically refugial, but climate change is closing this. *Akiapola'au and 'akepa surveys 2024 show declines below 1,700 m and stability above 1,900 m* (USGS Hakalau survey 1999–2024).

**Ecological case for aggressive private-property suppression: yes, unambiguously.** Killing *any* wild Culex on a private parcel removes potential infected vectors from the landscape — every Culex eliminated is one fewer possible bridge between an infected lowland 'amakihi (the lowland reservoir species, which has evolved partial resistance) and an upslope susceptible 'i'iwi or 'akiapola'au. Suppressing *Ae. albopictus* also reduces avian pox, which kills birds independently. The user's property is effectively a buffer zone for the NWR.

## 3. Wolbachia IIT / "Birds Not Mosquitoes" status (2025–2026)

The Birds Not Mosquitoes coalition (DLNR, USFWS, NPS, ABC, USGS) deploys the Incompatible Insect Technique: weekly aerial release of male *Culex quinquefasciatus* infected with an incompatible Wolbachia strain. Mating with wild females produces non-viable eggs.

- **Maui (Haleakalā NP):** Active since November 2023. ~500,000 males/week (Birds Not Mosquitoes: https://www.birdsnotmosquitoes.org/; NPS: https://www.nps.gov/articles/000/hale-rad.htm).
- **Kaua'i (Alaka'i Plateau):** Active. ~500,000/week (Kauai Forest Bird Recovery Project: https://kauaiforestbirds.org/mosquito-control/).
- **Big Island (Hakalau Forest NWR or Hawai'i Volcanoes NP):** **Planning phase.** April 2024 USFWS statement: "Over the next five years, the goal is to … complete the planning necessary to implement the technology at either Hakalau Forest National Wildlife Refuge or Hawai'i Volcanoes National Park" (FWS: https://www.fws.gov/story/2024-04/birds-brink). No active aerial releases on Hawai'i Island as of mid-2025; environmental assessment in progress (Hakalau Draft CCP-EA, March 2025: https://www.fws.gov/sites/default/files/documents/2025-03/hakalau-forest-draft-ccp-ea-8.9.10.pdf).

**Compatibility with private control: yes.** IIT works by saturating an area with sterile males — adding *more* female-killing pressure on the property complements it. Released males are targeted to landscape-level forest interiors at high elevation; private-property traps and barrier sprays operate near homes at lower elevations. Wolbachia males die naturally within ~1 week regardless. Killing wild females and breeding sites on a private parcel only improves the math.

**Coordination contact:** DOH Vector Control Branch (Hawaii Island has 15 dedicated VCB staff including an entomologist; statewide line 808-586-4708; https://health.hawaii.gov/vcb/) and USFWS Hakalau Forest NWR for higher-elevation parcels. Coordination is not legally required for routine homeowner mosquito suppression but is courteous if the parcel borders refuge land.

## 4. Regulatory and importation rules

- **Hawaii Department of Agriculture and Biosecurity (HDOA-DAB), Pesticides Branch** is the gatekeeper (https://dab.hawaii.gov/pi/pest/pesticides-rules-and-laws/). Phone 808-973-9560.
- **HRS Chapter 149A + Administrative Rules Title 4 Chapter 66** govern. Act 45 (2018) added pesticide use rules effective Jan 1, 2019.
- **General-use products** (mosquito dunks/Bti, most over-the-counter CO2 traps, off-the-shelf consumer pyrethroid sprays) — homeowner can apply on own property without license.
- **Bti (Bacillus thuringiensis israelensis):** General-use biopesticide. Sold widely (Mosquito Dunks brand etc.). No special HI license required for homeowner application. EPA-registered, accepted in HDOA's list (HISC: https://dlnr.hawaii.gov/hisc/mosquitos/).
- **Pyriproxyfen (insect growth regulator):** General-use for most consumer products (e.g., Nylar tablets); homeowner-applicable.
- **Bifenthrin (Talstar P / Bifen IT):** Listed in Hawaii's 2023 Restricted Use Pesticides Report (https://dab.hawaii.gov/pi/files/2021/04/2023-RUP-Use-Report.pdf). Most concentrate formulations are Restricted Use — require a certified applicator. Lower-concentration consumer formulations and pre-mixed barrier products may be general-use; verify the specific EPA registration before purchase.
- **In2Care Mosquito Station (pyriproxyfen + Beauveria bassiana, EPA Reg. 91720-1):** Per manufacturer, professional application is intended; "must possess a valid pest control license for professional application." Hawaii does not appear to block In2Care registration — but a 2022 Big Island field study of In2Care at Miloli'i had to be halted by DOH after the trial worsened populations due to too-low trap density and abundant cryptic competing breeding sites (Cabanas et al., *J. Med. Entomol.* 2023, https://academic.oup.com/jme/article/60/2/364/6992887). Lesson: **In2Care is not a standalone solution** without aggressive source reduction first.
- **CO2 propane traps (Mosquito Magnet, Biogents BG-Sentinel/BG-Mosquitaire):** General-merchandise devices; no Hawaii-specific shipping restriction. Propane is sourced locally.
- **Importation (HDOA Plant Quarantine):** Live biocontrol organisms (predatory copepods, *Toxorhynchites*) require permits. Chemical pesticides registered with HDOA ship freely.
- **Hawaii VCB (DOH) does not provide private-property treatment**; their role is regulatory and surveillance for arboviral disease. Citizens can report unusual mosquito issues at 808-586-4708.

- **Big Island Pest Control** (Kailua-Kona) — residential and commercial, including vacation rentals.
- **Western Exterminator / Rentokil** (statewide, https://www.westernexterminator.com/local-branches/hawaii) — Hawai'i-specific protocols.
- **Orkin Kamuela** (https://www.orkin.com/locations/hawaii-hi/kamuela-pest-control/branch-751) — corporate operation; standard Talstar-style barrier protocols.

Typical multi-acre mosquito-suppression program on Big Island: barrier spray every 21–30 days April–November, In2Care station network (typically 1 station per ~4,300 sq ft of treatment area), plus property walkthrough source-reduction audit. Annual cost typically runs $1,500–$5,000 for a residential acreage, scaling with frequency, station count, and forested acreage. (No Hawaii-published rate cards; based on national Mosquito Joe / Mosquito Authority rate ranges scaled for Hawaii premium.) Mosquito Joe does not appear to have a Big Island franchise; competitor coverage is via the named local firms above.

## 6. Bromeliad management (Wyeomyia and Aedes axil-breeding)

Bromeliads, heliconia tanks, and naturalized epiphytes are guaranteed *Wyeomyia mitchellii* habitat, and on Hawai'i — where *Wy. mitchellii* lacks competing native bromeliad mosquitoes — *Aedes albopictus* will also colonize axils (UF/IFAS https://edis.ifas.ufl.edu/publication/IN1343). Treatment options, ranked:

1. **Bti granules / dust into axils** — broadcast small amount into each tank every 14–21 days. Safe for plants, pets, and the rest of the bromeliad fauna. Recommended by University of Florida and Collier Mosquito Control District (https://cmcd.org/science/bromeliads-mosquitoes/).
2. **Pyriproxyfen tablet (e.g., Nylar) per axil** — longer residual (60–90 days). Works long-term but slower kill. General-use.
3. **Methoprene briquettes** — alternative IGR; similar to pyriproxyfen.
4. **Manual flush** with a hose every 5–7 days during dry-ish periods to disrupt larval cycle. Labor-intensive on >50 plants.
5. **Salt-water flush** — kills mosquitoes AND the bromeliad. Use only on plants you're willing to lose.
6. **Removal** — last resort, but warranted for high-density naturalized stands near the residence. Bromeliads further than ~150 m from outdoor living areas matter less since *Wy. mitchellii* has weak dispersal.

## 7. Microclimate / placement implications

- **No winter break.** Hakalau windward is frost-free; mosquito breeding is continuous. Treatment must be year-round, not seasonal.
- **Continuous wet substrate.** 150–250"+ rainfall means standing water in any container, gulch pool, leaf litter, palm boot, and Strawberry Guava root flare. Source reduction is the load-bearing intervention; chemical/biological control without source reduction performed badly in the Big Island In2Care trial.
- **Ohia + hapu'u fern + epiphyte canopy** holds water in countless cryptic axils. Aerial Bti application (drone-delivered or backpack-blower) is the practical canopy treatment; spot ground spraying misses the axils above 2 m.
- **Trade winds (NE prevailing).** Orient CO2 traps so the plume drifts *across* the area you want to protect — i.e., place traps on the windward (NE) edge of decks, dining areas, sleeping porches. A trap downwind of the protected zone is wasted.
- **Stream gulches and hapu'u depressions** are continuous Culex larval habitat; barrier sprays cannot reach them. Use Bti briquettes (30-day) or granules monthly in identified pools and axil clusters.
- **Elevation:** confirm parcel elevation. Below ~4,000 ft *Ae. albopictus* and Culex are both year-round; above ~5,000 ft Culex gets seasonal but is climbing. If parcel is mid-slope, treat as full-pressure year-round.

## Summary

A Hakalau parcel needs (a) aggressive source reduction (Bti everywhere water collects), (b) a year-round barrier-spray rotation or full-density In2Care/IGR network, (c) courtesy coordination with USFWS Hakalau Forest NWR if upslope-adjacent, and (d) recognition that aggressive private suppression *helps* IIT, not conflicts with it. The ecological framing — private suppression as honeycreeper recovery contribution — is real, not greenwashing.

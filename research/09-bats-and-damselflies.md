# Native predators on the Hakalau parcel: Hawaiian hoary bat + Megalagrion damselflies

Layer onto the trap/Bti suppression plan, also a conservation play. This doc verifies the popular advice ("mount bat boxes, eat 1000 mosquitoes/hr"), corrects what's wrong, and lays out what actually works on a 15-acre Hamakua coast parcel with a river edge and an existing small pond.

## TL;DR

- **Bat boxes are wrong for this species.** The Hawaiian hoary bat (ʻōpeʻapeʻa) is a foliage-roosting bat that almost never uses artificial roosts. Skip them entirely.
- **Bats won't eradicate mosquitoes.** Hawaiian hoary bat diet is dominated by moths and beetles; mosquitoes are a minor component. The "1000+ mosquitoes/hour" stat circulating online is from generic North American small-bat studies and does not apply to the Hawaiian hoary bat in any meaningful way.
- **Damselflies are the bigger native lever.** *Megalagrion xanthomelas* (orangeblack Hawaiian damselfly, federally endangered) is documented 5 miles south at Onomea Bay; it likely already uses the Hakalau Stream system. Damselfly larvae are voracious mosquito-larva predators in standing water.
- **No permit needed to "host" bats or damselflies.** Passive habitat enhancement is unregulated. The only hard rule: do NOT trim or cut woody plants ≥15 ft tall between **June 1 and September 15** (pupping season). That's federal under the ESA.
- **A formal "sanctuary" designation does not exist** in Hawaii law in the way people imagine. The closest legal instrument is a voluntary Safe Harbor Agreement (SHA) with DLNR/USFWS, useful only if you'll be developing structures and want incidental-take assurance.
- **Combined mosquito-control contribution: probably 5-15%.** The 27-trap Biogents plan + Bti remains the load-bearing intervention. The bats and damselflies are a conservation + brand win, not a replacement for engineered suppression.

## 1. Hawaiian hoary bat (ʻōpeʻapeʻa) — reality check on the popular advice

### Status

- Federally endangered under the U.S. Endangered Species Act (1970).
- State-listed endangered under HRS Chapter 195D.
- The only land mammal native to Hawaiʻi.
- Population: "a few hundred to a few thousand" — no precise estimate exists.

Sources: [USFWS species profile](https://ecos.fws.gov/ecp/species/770), [Wikipedia — Hawaiian hoary bat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawaiian_hoary_bat), [Bat Conservation International](https://www.batcon.org/bat/hawaiian-hoary-bat/).

### Diet — moths, not mosquitoes

The first genetic stomach-content analysis (USGS Waihou Mitigation Area study, Maui) found:

- **Moths (Lepidoptera): primary prey** by both prevalence and gene-sequence count
- Beetles (Coleoptera): secondary
- Mosquitoes, midges, termites, crickets, true bugs, cockroaches: minor components

In open environments moths dominate; in cluttered understory the diet diversifies but never becomes mosquito-dominated.

The "consumes 1,000 mosquitoes per hour" claim is from generic mainland small-bat marketing copy, not Hawaiian hoary bat data. The 2013 PLOS ONE study "Mosquito Consumption by Insectivorous Bats: Does Size Matter?" specifically warns that body size limits mosquito consumption. The Hawaiian hoary bat is mid-sized (forearm 47-56mm) and is observably a moth specialist.

Sources: [USGS Waihou diet study](https://www.usgs.gov/publications/hawaiian-hoary-bat-lasiurus-cinereus-semotus-activity-diet-and-prey-availability), [PLOS ONE — Mosquito consumption by bats](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0077183).

### Roosting — foliage, not boxes

Hawaiian hoary bats roost in **tree foliage**, day-roosting alone or as mother-pup pairs. Documented roost trees include: ʻōhiʻa, hala (pu hala), coconut palm, kukui, kiawe, avocado, shower tree, pūkiawe, fern clumps, eucalyptus, cook pine, and Norfolk Island pine. They prefer:

- Roost height 3-5 m above ground (sometimes up to 26 m)
- Dense leaf cover ABOVE the roost
- Open flight space BELOW
- Trees bordering clearings (edge habitat)

USGS notes "low roost lability" — bats use a small set of preferred roosts and return to them. They do NOT readily occupy artificial structures, lava tubes, or rock crevices except rarely.

**Implication: bat boxes don't work for this species.** Don't buy them. Spend the money on planting/preserving native canopy instead.

Sources: [USGS Hawaiʻi Island ʻōpeʻapeʻa roost data 2018-2021](https://www.usgs.gov/data/hawaii-island-opeapea-hawaiian-hoary-bat-roost-data-2018-2021), [DLNR ʻŌpeʻapeʻa page](https://dlnr.hawaii.gov/wildlife/opeapea/).

### Home range — your 15 acres is part of ONE bat's territory

USGS telemetry data:

- Mean core-use area: ~63 acres (25.5 ha), with little overlap between individuals
- Males: 84.3 acres core area
- Females: 41.2 acres core area

Your 15-acre parcel is roughly **18-36% of a single bat's core area.** You cannot "host" a population on this footprint; you contribute to a landscape-scale population that uses Hakalau Stream corridor + Hakalau Forest NWR + the surrounding Hamakua mosaic.

Sources: [USGS — Ecology and distribution of the endangered Hawaiian hoary bat](https://www.usgs.gov/pacific-island-ecosystems-research-center/science/ecology-and-distribution-endangered-hawaiian).

### Are they on/near the property already?

Almost certainly yes.

- Bat habitat ranges from sea level to 7,507 ft. Activity peaks at LOW elevations (below 1,280 m / 4,200 ft) **April through December** — exactly when bats are pregnant, lactating, and rearing pups in lowland warm-season habitat. The parcel sits at coastal Hakalau elevation (low hundreds of feet), squarely in the lowland reproductive zone.
- Acoustic surveys ran at Hakalau Forest NWR and Laupāhoehoe Forest Reserve at 1,069-1,200 m elevation in 2007-2013, recording confirmed Hawaiian hoary bat echolocation. The species is documented up-slope on the same Mauna Kea face the parcel sits below.
- Mist-net captures of bats happened at "multiple locations on the east side of Hawaiʻi Island" 2018-2021 (USGS).

Get a baseline acoustic recording on the property — cheap with an Echo Meter Touch 2 ($179) or full-spectrum SM4BAT logger (~$700) — to confirm.

Sources: [USGS Hakalau acoustic data 2007-2013](https://www.usgs.gov/data/hawaii-island-hawaiian-hoary-bat-acoustic-data-pre-and-post-koa-moth-outbreak-2007-2013), [USGS east Hawaiʻi mist-net data](https://www.usgs.gov/data/hawaii-island-opeapea-hawaiian-hoary-bat-lasiurus-semotus-mist-netting-effort-capture-and-tag).

## 2. What "host bats" actually means on this property

### The hard regulatory rule

**Do not trim, cut, or remove woody plants ≥15 ft tall between June 1 and September 15 each year.**

Female bats give birth in mid-June; pups are flightless until late August/early September. Trimming a tall tree during pupping season risks killing pups that physically cannot escape. This is enforced under the federal ESA "take" prohibition. Plan all tall-tree maintenance for **October through May**.

If you must remove a tree during pupping season for safety reasons, contact USFWS Pacific Islands Office and DLNR DOFAW first.

Sources: [Navy Region Hawaii — pupping season guidance](https://cnrh.cnic.navy.mil/News/News-Detail/Article/3472184/protecting-the-hawaiian-hoary-bat-during-pupping-season/), [USFWS Avoidance and Minimization Measures (May 2023)](https://www.fws.gov/sites/default/files/documents/Animal%20Avoidance%20and%20Minimization%20Measures%20-%20FINAL%20May%202023_0.pdf).

### Habitat enhancements that don't need permits

- **Plant or preserve native canopy trees:** ʻōhiʻa lehua (*Metrosideros polymorpha*), kukui (*Aleurites moluccanus*), hala (*Pandanus tectorius*). All are well-suited to Hakalau windward conditions. Group them in clusters near the retreat structures so bats can forage and you can witness it.
- **Maintain edge habitat:** keep the boundary between landscaped and forested areas — bats forage along edges.
- **Skip pesticides that kill moths.** The mosquito-control plan in this repo (CO2 traps, Bti, In2Care) is species-targeted; that's compatible. Avoid generic landscape pyrethroid fogging that wipes out moths.
- **Remove barbed wire.** Hawaiian hoary bats die snagged on barbed-wire fences — multiple documented mortalities. If any exists on the parcel, replace with smooth wire or hog/post-and-rail.
- **Outdoor lighting:** minimize total brightness; use warm-temp (≤2700K) or amber/red wavelengths near tree clusters. Bright white floodlights disorient nocturnal foraging and attract moths to a "sink" away from natural patches.
- **Leave dead branches and snags where safe.** Hoary bats sometimes day-roost on partial canopy snags.

### Habitat features to AVOID

- Wind turbines (lethal for hoary bats — the leading documented mortality cause).
- Bright security lighting around mature trees during pupping season.
- Mass-clearing or aggressive understory removal.
- Pesticide fogging (any).
- Barbed wire.

Sources: [DLNR ʻŌpeʻapeʻa page](https://dlnr.hawaii.gov/wildlife/opeapea/), [DLNR Hoary Bat Guidance Document](https://dlnr.hawaii.gov/wildlife/esrc/hhb/).

## 3. "Sanctuary" status — what you actually need

There is no Hawaiian "private bat sanctuary" license. People conflate three distinct things:

| Instrument | What it is | When you need it | Cost / effort |
|---|---|---|---|
| Informal "wildlife sanctuary" (brand) | Marketing/signage. No legal status. | Anytime. Just be honest about what habitat you have. | Free. |
| **Safe Harbor Agreement (SHA)** | Voluntary contract with DLNR/USFWS. Establishes a baseline of endangered species; new individuals attracted by your habitat work do NOT trigger additional regulation if you later need to disturb them. ([HRS §195D-22](https://law.justia.com/codes/hawaii/2013/title-12/chapter-195d/section-195d-22/)) | If you plan future construction (new lodging, gym expansion, structures) and want assurance against ESA-take liability if more bats move in due to your habitat improvements. | Application + biological survey. Months of negotiation. Coordinator: DLNR DOFAW Conservation Initiatives, (808) 587-0166. |
| Habitat Conservation Plan (HCP) / Incidental Take License | Required if your activity WILL take/kill listed species — e.g., wind turbines, large clearing. | Not applicable to a passive retreat. Only relevant if you build a wind farm or do large-scale clearing. | Significant — multi-year process, biologist contracts, BLNR approval. |

**For your case (15-acre retreat doing passive habitat enhancement): no permit. No SHA needed yet.** Revisit SHA only if/when you're planning major new construction within 5-10 years.

Sources: [DLNR Safe Harbor Agreements](https://dlnr.hawaii.gov/wildlife/hcp/safe-harbor/), [HRS §195D-23 incentives](https://law.justia.com/codes/hawaii/title-12/chapter-195d/section-195d-23/), [Sand County Foundation SHA assessment](https://sandcountyfoundation.org/uploads/Foreword-Safe-Harbor-FINAL.pdf).

### Free landowner support — actually use it

- **DOFAW Hawaiʻi Island Branch:** can do free property-level wildlife assessments. Conservation initiatives staff at (808) 587-0166.
- **USFWS Pacific Islands Office:** advisory consultations free for landowners.
- **DLNR ʻŌpeʻapeʻa sighting map** (launched July 2025): you can submit observations through the online form. Useful for both research and marketing. ([DLNR press release 7/29/25](https://dlnr.hawaii.gov/blog/2025/07/29/nr25-101/))

## 4. Megalagrion damselflies — the actual native mosquito predator

23 endemic Hawaiian damselflies in the genus *Megalagrion* — pinapinao in ʻōlelo Hawaiʻi. **Six are federally listed endangered.** Larvae are aquatic predators eating mosquito larvae, midges, small invertebrates. Adults eat flying mosquitoes, midges, and other small flying insects.

### Species likely on or near the parcel

| Species | Status | Habitat | Likelihood at Hakalau |
|---|---|---|---|
| *M. xanthomelas* (orangeblack) | Federal endangered (2016) | 0-2,000 ft elevation, slow streams, coastal wetlands, anchialine pools | **High.** Documented at Onomea Bay and Hilo (USFWS species report). Onomea Bay is ~5 miles south of Hakalau on the same coast. |
| *M. hawaiiense* (Hawaiian damselfly) | Not federally listed, common | Lowland streams + plunge pools island-wide | **Likely already in Hakalau Stream.** |
| *M. nigrohamatum nigrolineatum* | Federal endangered | Stream habitats | Lower likelihood at this elevation, but possible. |
| *M. nesiotes* | Federal endangered | Higher-elevation streams | Unlikely at coastal parcel; possible upslope. |

A dusk walk along the river with a phone-camera photo grab is the fastest baseline survey. Even untrained eyes can recognize Megalagrion: bright red, orange, black, or red+black coloration; perched on streamside vegetation.

Sources: [USFWS *M. xanthomelas* species report](https://ecos.fws.gov/docs/recovery_plan/Megalagrion%20xanthomelas_Species%20Report_Final.pdf), [Xerces Society *M. xanthomelas*](https://xerces.org/endangered-species/species-profiles/at-risk-aquatic-invertebrates/orangeblack-hawaiian-damselfly), [DLNR Pinapinao page](https://dlnr.hawaii.gov/ecosystems/hip/species/pinapinao/).

### Habitat needs (and the one fatal mistake to avoid)

What native Megalagrion damselflies need:

- **Fish-free water.** Introduced poeciliids (mosquitofish *Gambusia affinis*, guppies, swordtails) eat damselfly larvae. Mosquitofish are the **single biggest cause of Megalagrion decline** statewide. ([Englund 1999, *J. Insect Conservation*](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/A:1009651922486))
- **Streamside or pond-edge native vegetation** for adult perching and oviposition. Hawaiian sedges (*Carex* spp.), kalo (taro) along edges, native ferns, hāpuʻu fronds.
- **Slow water sections** — plunge pools, eddies, the slow tail of pond outlets.
- **Some sun exposure.** Adults need warmed perches; they're not deep-shade species.
- **Connection to surrounding stream** — naturally colonizes if Hakalau Stream upstream/downstream has populations.

The fatal mistake — and the most common landscape-design "mosquito control" advice — is to stock ponds with mosquitofish. Do not do this at Hakalau. It will:
- Eliminate any native damselfly larvae immediately
- Eliminate native shrimp (ʻōpae) in connected waters
- Eat tadpoles of any future native frog efforts (none currently in HI, but moot)
- Provide mediocre mosquito control (mosquitofish prefer soft-bodied invertebrates over mosquito larvae when both present)

Source: [USFWS — Western Mosquitofish ecological risk screening](https://www.fws.gov/sites/default/files/documents/2024-06/ecological-risk-screening-summary-western-mosquitofish.pdf).

### What about the existing pond?

The site doc describes a "small pond, exists, un-upgraded" with a future-purpose as natural hot-spring-style pool.

Conflict: a heated/treated/chlorinated soaking pool is incompatible with damselfly habitat. Pick one.

Two cleaner solutions:

**(A) Convert the existing pond to native wildlife habitat.** Build the soaking pool elsewhere. Pond becomes a fish-free shallow basin with sedge edges, native ferns, slow circulation from a stream tap if feasible. Cost: low. Brand benefit: high.

**(B) Add 2-3 small "wildlife pools" elsewhere on the parcel.** Rocky shallow basins, 1-3 m diameter, sited near the river and partially shaded. Native plants on edges. Stream-fed if the topography allows; otherwise rain-filled with seasonal cycle. Function as both Megalagrion breeding habitat AND visual landscape features at the retreat. Cost: $1-3K each in plants + minor earthwork.

Either way: zero introduced fish in any pond. Aerate against mosquito breeding via a small solar pump (~$50) if needed; agitation alone disrupts *Aedes/Culex* oviposition.

## 5. Reintroduction or natural colonization?

**Don't try to import damselflies.** Translocation is being trialed by DLNR / USGS / Bishop Museum at permitted sites only ([Maui Now 2019](https://mauinow.com/2019/05/31/damselflies-in-distress-get-help-at-island-pond/), [DLNR 2021 Dillingham reintroduction](https://dlnr.hawaii.gov/blog/2021/05/07/nr21-086/)) and is permit-gated.

If suitable habitat appears AND nearby populations exist (likely: Hakalau Stream, Onomea Bay), Megalagrion will colonize naturally. They disperse along stream corridors and on wind. Build it; they come.

**To accelerate:** contact UH Hilo Department of Biology (Steven Montgomery / Dan Polhemus / Ron Englund were the historical Hawaii Megalagrion specialists; check current faculty), or the Bishop Museum entomology collection. They sometimes do paid surveys on private lands and are interested in lowland Hawaiʻi Island sites.

## 6. Honest math: combined mosquito-control contribution

| Intervention | Dominant prey | Mosquito impact | Conservation value |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hawaiian hoary bat (foraging on parcel) | Moths, beetles | Negligible | High |
| *Megalagrion* damselflies (in fish-free pools) | Mosquito larvae + midges + small inverts | Local, in-pool only | Very high |
| 27 × Biogents BG-Mosquitaire CO2 (in plan) | Adult mosquitoes (CO2-attracted) | **Primary** | None |
| Bti larvicide (in plan) | Mosquito larvae landscape-wide | **Major** | High (no off-target effects) |
| In2Care stations + barrier (in plan) | Adults + autodissemination | Major | Low to moderate |

**Realistic estimate:** native predators contribute 5-15% of total mosquito reduction at most. The trap+Bti plan in `02-commercial-traps-and-math.md` and `04-integrated-suppression.md` does 85%+. Don't reduce the trap count to "let nature handle it."

The conservation case is independent. Hawaiian hoary bat presence and Megalagrion populations on a working retreat are:

- A real ESA-listed-species recovery contribution
- A material brand asset (genuine, defensible, unusual)
- Compatible with everything else in the suppression plan

That's the right framing.

## 7. Phased plan

### Phase 1: free, this calendar year
- Dusk-to-night walk of river + pond, 30-min sessions, 2-3 nights. Photograph any damselflies; note locations. Listen for bat echolocation (audible click range with phone-mic ultrasonic apps like Echo Meter Touch demo).
- Map every tree ≥15 ft on the parcel. Mark on a parcel survey. Schedule any planned trims for **October-May only.**
- Pull any barbed wire on perimeter; replace with smooth-wire or post-and-rail.
- Audit outdoor lighting near tree clusters; switch any white-LED floods to warm/amber.
- Add the existing river + pond to the "no mosquitofish, ever" rule. Flag in onboarding for future contractors.

### Phase 2: $1-3K, Year 1-2
- Plant native pond + river-edge species: Hawaiian sedges (*Carex wahuensis*), kalo at margins, native ferns, ʻākulikuli (*Sesuvium portulacastrum*) where damp/saline.
- Convert the existing small pond to fish-free wildlife pool. Build soaking pool elsewhere.
- Plant 2-3 ʻōhiʻa or kukui clusters within sight of retreat structures, accepting they take 5-10 years to reach >15 ft canopy.
- Get a baseline bat acoustic survey: 1 SM4BAT logger or Echo Meter Touch ($200-700), 5-7 nights. Confirms presence and gives a defensible brand stat.

### Phase 3: $10-30K + ongoing, Year 2-5
- Build a 2-3 unit "damselfly pool network" near the river — small fish-free rocky basins with native plant edges. Function as landscape AND habitat. Contact UH Hilo entomology for site evaluation; they may co-fund or co-author.
- If new structures planned, open conversation with DLNR DOFAW about Safe Harbor Agreement.
- Continuous acoustic monitoring (1 fixed station at retreat structure cluster).
- Submit ʻŌpeʻapeʻa observations to DLNR online sighting map.

### Phase 4: brand integration (free)
- Onsite signage / retreat program element: short interpretive section on Megalagrion + ʻōpeʻapeʻa, guests learn what they're seeing.
- Annual native species report bundled with retreat marketing.
- Potential DLNR landowner recognition (HRS §195D-23 incentives include annual recognition for sound conservation practices).

## 8. Things to watch for

- **Avian malaria + Birds Not Mosquitoes coordination.** The IIT (Wolbachia) program on Hawaiʻi Island is in planning phase and will likely launch from Hakalau Forest NWR (refer to `03-hakalau-specifics.md`). Suppressing Culex on the parcel and hosting native predators are both compatible with IIT.
- **Climate creep on bat habitat.** As Culex elevation creeps up, the bat low-elevation reproductive zone overlaps with the highest-pressure mosquito zone. Anything that restores native canopy at this elevation is doubly valuable.
- **Liability.** Federal ESA "take" prohibition is criminal. Educate any landscape contractors on the June 1-September 15 tree-cut rule before they cut anything ≥15 ft tall. Get this in writing in any service contract.
- **Don't oversell the bats.** Public-facing material can say "endangered Hawaiian hoary bat foraging habitat." Don't claim a colony or count; populations are loose and individuals are mostly solitary.
